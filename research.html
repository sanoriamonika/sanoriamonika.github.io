Research

I study how tiny moving particles, like cells or artificial active particles, interact and organize themselves. My research focuses on understanding how these particles move, cluster, and sometimes separate under different conditions. I explore how softness, external forces, and surrounding environments influence their collective behavior. One of my key areas of interest is motility‑induced phase separation (MIPS), where moving particles spontaneously form clusters. I have shown that when particles are soft, they can create porous, connected structures instead of just dense clusters. Another major focus is on how cancerous cell clusters respond to chemical signals and move together, which is important for understanding diseases. My work combines physics, biology, and computational models to uncover new principles governing self‑organization in active matter systems.

Focus Areas

  • Motility‑Induced Phase Separation (MIPS)
  • Percolation transitions in active matter
  • Soft active particles and porous clustering
  • Collective cell migration and chemotaxis in cancer
  • Computational & theoretical biophysics of self‑organization
