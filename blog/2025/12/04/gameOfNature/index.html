Insight: Game of nature

Patterns as Our First Language

December 04, 2025 • Updated December 04, 2025

Short Summary:

We enter life knowing nothing, yet we slowly discover hidden patterns that govern survival—hunger, danger, desire, and belonging. By watching others, we realize these patterns shape belief systems, choices, and the entire cycle of life. From ancient cells forming alliances to modern societies sharing knowledge, survival has always scaled through collaboration. And now, with AI merging collective intelligence, we may be entering the next level of this “game.” The question is: how much of it do we truly understand?

The Game We Enter Without Knowing

What do we know about this world? Nothing. I was somehow a child, then mysteriously my body changed, and things around me continuously changed. It seems to happen linearly, and this effect is called time. I consume things around me that my body craves. There are some people who brought me here, and other beings who helped me adjust to this world. I see that some of those people look very different, their skin becomes loose, they walk differently, and slowly they cannot fulfill the needs of their body. Eventually their body stops functioning altogether. After that, there is no movement or any function that existed before.

Everything has a pattern. Some people develop saggy skin, a bent walking posture, and eventually stop all activities; at that point, their bodies are either burned or buried. Another pattern: someone is born, reaches childhood, adulthood, middle age, old age, and then dies. Have I seen one person go through all stages fully? No. But because I have seen different people at different points, I can extrapolate that this is the cycle.

We see that our bodies force us to eat. Everyone cries or becomes restless if not fed. We eat plants, animals, and fruits to curb hunger. After eating, we become calmer and able to behave normally. When there is hunger, humans or animals are ruled by that feeling more than any other sense. Sexual desire is similar. When the desire arises, it takes over behavior, and people behave differently. Once that need is fulfilled, the mind becomes free to think more clearly. Similarly, when danger is present, the feeling of protecting ourselves dominates everything. Until danger passes, the system does not function calmly. These seem to be priorities. A difficult question is: if hunger, danger, and sexual desire arise together, which one would a human satisfy first?

Where we live, we have seen behaviors around us, and naturally we feel more comfortable satisfying these needs in a particular order. Maybe because, from patterns, we assume that if many people act in a certain way, it is probably the best way to survive. There is comfort in patterns. Our beliefs are formed through patterns. What is a belief system? Something that has not harmed us yet and seemed useful becomes accepted as right. There is safety in repeating patterns. When someone else’s patterns have worked reliably for many years, we are inclined to follow them. We saw that needs were fulfilled for those who studied and worked; we wanted to do the same because it increased the chances of our safety and the safety of those trying to ensure our survival. So, survival became another subtle game behind hunger, sex, and danger.

We play this game from subtle needs to strong needs by learning patterns through observing others. Better pattern recognition improves survival. Every understanding feels solid until new information arrives and gives more clarity. It is like in a video game. A player suddenly finds themselves in a world; the aim becomes survival. Out of an entire population, maybe half decide to survive and half do not. We only observe the half that survive, so we assume survival is the main purpose. The question becomes: how long will it take for the player to figure out the rules of the game? I, like others, have been thrown into this game, and without knowing it, I play it by looking at patterns others follow. Better pattern recognition, better survival.

So what have I understood about the game? Collective Existence Improves Survival

Collectiveness has consistently helped survival and helped develop better pattern recognition. This is true across scales from cells, to animals, to humans. Whoever found the next cue survived. Whoever adapted to new environments survived. There were no fixed rules; the method did not matter. A factual example: Cells and mitochondria originally existed as separate organisms. Over time, through mutually beneficial interactions, mitochondria were absorbed into larger host cells and began living symbiotically. Today, every human cell contains mitochondria, and mitochondrial DNA is inherited exclusively from the mother. To overcome increasing survival challenges, individual cells eventually formed multicellular communities, which then gave rise to tissues, organs, and coordinated systems. This evolutionary pathway ultimately led to the complex multicellular organisms we see today, including humans.

Humans learned individually (e.g., hunting alone), then collectively (farming), which increased stability and safety. With scale, villages emerged, then cities. Religion united thousands under shared beliefs which is another scaling mechanism. A major milestone was the idea of codifying and publishing knowledge, allowing future generations and even different countries to work on the same problems. This dramatically increased the scale of problem-solving. Now countries work simultaneously on the same ideas, trying not to fall behind.

Has this reduced problems? No, we now have more problems and more solutions, but in focused directions—directions that survived curiosity, necessity, or the test of time. Now AI collects past knowledge and accelerates idea development. This feels like another scaling layer. Sometimes I wonder: is this scaling following a power-law pattern?
