The Cell Surface and Adhesion Machinery

In addition to these internal actomyosin structures, the cell membrane has functional proteins (cell surface proteins) that include signaling proteins, transport proteins, and adhesion molecules.

These cell adhesion molecules (CAMs) mediate binding to either the extracellular matrix (ECM) or other cells (cell junctions).

CAMs are mainly divided into four categories depending on the type of interaction:

  • Immunoglobulin Superfamily (IgSF CAMs) — cell–cell and cell–ECM binding (calcium-independent)

  • Cadherins — cell–cell binding, especially in epithelial tissues (calcium-dependent)

  • Selectins — cell–cell binding in immune cells (calcium-dependent)

  • Integrins — cell–ECM binding (calcium-dependent)

How these molecules form bonds and how long they remain connected is still an active area of research.

Polarity and Complexity

Cells also exhibit polarity — they are not circularly symmetric. One side acts as the front, the opposite as the back, and the adhesion and surface dynamics differ drastically between the two ends. This adds yet another layer of complexity to cell–cell interactions.

Recent work of these authors aims to understand what happens when cells interact with each other (PNAS, 2021) and which types of interactions dominate such encounters (PRX Life, 2025). They created a cell collider (see Video S5 (PNAS)) and studied the trajectories of these cells and their goal to infer some sort of scholastic trajectories law that can explain their collision motion more previously with the hope that it could be used sometime in collective dynamics to get better information about the system.
