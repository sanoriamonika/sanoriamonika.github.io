Paper Summary: Data-Driven Theory Reveals Protrusion and Polarity Interactions Governing Collision Behavior of Distinct Motile Cells

October 06, 2025

Author Tom Brandstätter, extending the work of David Brückner from the lab of Chase P. Broedersz, explores what happens when cells collide with each other (For more detail read post). The study investigates which types of forces dominate during these interactions — whether they arise from nucleus–nucleus, protrusion–protrusion, or polarity–polarity interactions.

The authors show that, out of all the proposed interaction candidates, polarity–protrusion and polarity–polarity couplings are the most significant in explaining how cells behave during collisions.

Interestingly, by simply changing the sign or strength of the polarity–polarity interaction within the same model, they can reproduce the transition from non-cancerous to cancer-like cell cluster behavior — suggesting that these interaction rules may underlie fundamental differences between healthy and malignant cell types.

Reflections

  • How does a cell choose its polarity? Can we have another data-based study to identify some generalized principles for cell polarity as well?
