Review Summary: Endocytosis in the context-dependent regulation of individual and collective cell properties

October 01, 2025

Endocytosis CellCommunication ERK Cadherin Polarity ReviewSummary

Cells eat and communicate with their environment — but how do they actually do that? What does it even mean for a cell to “eat”? We humans eat for many reasons — taste, pleasure, or simply habit — but cells don’t eat for taste. Not at all :)

Cells eat to communicate, to produce energy, and to stay alive.

It’s mind-blowing to realize that every single cell has this kind of built-in awareness — a biochemical form of consciousness that keeps it in constant conversation with its surroundings.

In our bodies, about 37 trillion cells are doing this every moment, in harmony with around 100 trillion microbes living inside us. Together, they form an intricate, self-regulating ecosystem — a community that balances survival, cooperation, and communication at microscopic scales.

Now, cells don’t have a hard, fixed boundary. Their outer layer, the cell membrane, is more like a magical, flexible sheet made of lipids and proteins. The lipids form a bilayer (see Fig.1), and the embedded proteins act like tiny gates or pores (see Fig.2)— letting small molecules slip through, depending on concentration differences, while keeping larger ones out.

When a cell needs to take in something larger — like nutrients or signaling molecules — it performs endocytosis (see Fig.1(b)).

It’s almost as if the cell membrane wraps around the target, enclosing it like a blanket, then pinches off to bring it inside (see Fig.1 and 3). This process is often the first step in signaling — a way for the cell to sense what’s around it. Once inside, specialized molecules decode that information, process it, and decide what to do next. Finally, after the cell has used what it needs, it expels the remaining material back out through exocytosis — completing a continuous loop of exchange and communication.

Endocytosis is an umbrella term that includes several distinct types of cellular uptake processes, as shown in the image below (see Fig.3).

What I understand is that endocytosis is not just a process that suppresses some signals while allowing others to pass through. It plays a far deeper role — acting as a form of communication between the cell and its environment, and even among neighboring cells.

One of the best-understood examples is clathrin-mediated endocytosis, where, either due to membrane fluctuations or specific signaling cues, the cell membrane forms a small invagination. At this site, clathrin molecules assemble along with adaptor proteins and receptors that determine which molecules will be internalized.

Eventually, this pit pinches off with the help of dynamin, forming a clathrin-coated vesicle that carries its cargo inside the cell. Other pathways exist too — clathrin-independent and caveolae-mediated endocytosis — where different proteins replace clathrin as the structural scaffold, allowing cells to internalize materials under different mechanical or signaling conditions.

A classic example of signaling linked to endocytosis is the ERK pathway. Traditionally, it was believed that such signaling occurs only at the cell surface. However, this work shows that receptors can continue to signal after endocytosis, from within the cell’s interior. I imagine it as receptors at the surface binding their ligands, being internalized, and then continuing to communicate with the nucleus from inside the cytoplasm. Because these internal vesicles persist longer, they maintain signaling over extended times and distances — adding both spatial and temporal depth to how the cell decides whether to divide, grow, differentiate, or migrate.

Another striking idea in this work is the role of endocytosis in cell polarity and collective migration. In migrating clusters, endocytosis is higher at the leading edge, where cells explore and move forward, and lower at the rear, where adhesions keep the tissue intact. Changes in endocytosis at the rear are transmitted to neighboring cells through cadherin-based junctions, creating a feedback that allows the entire cluster to adjust adhesions and move cohesively.

The authors suggest that this continuous remodeling — breaking and reforming cell–cell contacts smoothly rather than abruptly — is what allows tissues to migrate as coherent, living collectives. Endocytosis, therefore, emerges not just as a gateway for material exchange, but as a subtle and powerful mechanism of communication, coordination, and balance at the heart of cellular life.

Reflections

In our experiments, we observed that single cells exposed to a strong chemoattractant gradient often show chemorepulsion rather than attraction. Interestingly, when endocytosis was inhibited, this chemorepulsive behavior disappeared. This led us to ask whether endocytosis plays a direct role in establishing or maintaining cell polarity. Since endocytosis regulates the internalization and recycling of surface receptors, blocking it likely disrupts the cell’s ability to spatially reorganize signaling components that define its front and rear. As a result, the cell can no longer reverse its polarity in response to steep gradients, leading to the loss of chemorepulsion.

We conclude that endocytosis is not merely a transport mechanism but a key regulator of polarity-dependent responses, linking receptor trafficking to the directional decision-making of cells.

  • Fun fact: Most cells have a single lipid bilayer forming their outer membrane, but the nucleus is surrounded by a double bilayer — an inner and outer membrane.

  • Organelles involved in processing endocytosed vesicles, such as endosomes and lysosomes, have a single lipid bilayer as well.
