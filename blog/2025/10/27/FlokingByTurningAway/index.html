Paper Summary: Flocking by Turning Away

October 27, 2025 • Updated November 03, 2025

Two toys come close and see each other and they instantly turn away, since they don’t like each other. As a result, the whole system looks quite random, with everyone facing in different directions.

Now imagine we increase the density of such “toys.” One toy sees another on the left and tries to turn right, but at the same time sees someone on the right and want to turns left. These opposite rotations cancel out, so each toy ends up moving roughly straight ahead. And that’s how alignment actually emerges from pure chemorepulsion.. funny, isn’t it?That’s the system!

As you can see from the above example, density plays a crucial role. At low density, individual toys (or Janus particles) have enough space to freely turn away from one another, leading to a disordered, randomly oriented state.
But as the density increases, mutual interactions start to interfere and each one’s attempt to turn away is counteracted by another neighbor doing the same.
This frustration in turning eventually leads to collective alignment, where the group as a whole starts moving coherently even though the underlying interaction is purely repulsive.

Using computational modeling, the authors described the system and explained its properties, connecting it to known classes of active matter. They treated each Janus particle as an asymmetric dipole because it has two chemically different hemispheres in which one metallic and one dielectric, which create self-propulsion and anisotropic interactions under an AC electric field.

They found that at low density the system is disordered, but at intermediate density it exhibits clear flocking.
As the density increases further, the system transitions first into a hexatic flock (short-range orientational order) and then into a flocking crystal called as Wigner crystals (long-range positional order).

To pinpoint the microscopic origin of this order, they analyzed pair correlations divided into four angular quadrants.
Particles showed strong correlations in the first and fourth quadrants, meaning that the second particle prefers to stay in the angular range $ -\pi/2 < \theta < \pi/2 $ i.e., mostly in front or slightly to the sides.
This bias breaks the rotational symmetry of the “isotropic” state and seeds alignment. To identify whether flocking arises mainly from repulsive forces or from torque interactions, they compared simulations with and without repulsion.
When only the turning torque was present, the alignment order parameter remained zero — particles simply rotated away without forming any collective direction.
When both repulsion and torque were present, flocking appeared.
In that regime, repulsion prevents particles from approaching too closely, so they separate before completing a full rotation.
This makes the outgoing orientation difference smaller than the incoming one ($|\theta_{\text{out}}| < |\theta_{\text{in}}|$).
That incomplete turning caused by repulsion acting faster than rotation creates the asymmetry that leads to global order.
In short, the presence of chemorepulsion actually enhances flocking by turning avoidance into alignment.

Reflections

  • This paper demonstrates that flocking can emerge even when particles turn away from each other and alignment is not always required as an explicit rule.
  • It provides a physical explanation for how repulsive and antisymmetric torques can spontaneously produce coherent motion.

Citation

Authors: Suchismita Das, Matteo Ciarchi, Ziqi Zhou, Jing Yan, Jie Zhang, and Ricard Alert
Published in: Physical Review X, July 2024
DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevX.14.031008
