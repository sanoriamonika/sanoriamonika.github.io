Paper Summary: Self-assembled active actomyosin gels spontaneously curve and wrinkle similar to biological cells and tissues

October 16, 2025

Contractile InVitro ShapeDeformation Actomyosin Actin Myosin Fascin PoroElasticity PaperSummary

Actin filaments are long chains that continuously assemble and disassemble through a dynamic process of polymerization and depolymerization. They are made up of spherical G‑actin protein units, which polymerize using ATP (converted to ADP), a behavior well‑established from in vivo experiments. These filaments interact with myosin proteins, which have two globular heads connected by twisted filamentous tails. Myosin motors “walk” along the actin filaments by cyclically attaching to and detaching from the G‑actin subunits.

Together with intermediate filaments and microtubules, actin filaments form the cytoskeletal network of the cell, a fluid‑like yet structurally supportive system that maintains cell shape and holds the organelles in place (Watch Cytoskeleton;Sketchy MCAT and Cell Organelles: Drew Berry). Among these, microtubules are the largest filaments, actin filaments are the smallest, and the others of intermediate size are called intermediate filaments. When myosin motors move along actin filaments, they generate contractile forces, which drive cell migration, protrusion formation, cell division and membrane shaping. Because actomyosin contraction is one of the strongest mechanisms for converting chemical energy (ATP → ADP) into mechanical motion, it forms the basis of cellular motility and remains a fascinating topic of study.

There have been many in vivo and in vitro studies that examine this aspect of actomyosin. In this paper, the authors study a simple system, a pure actomyosin gel, in an in vitro experiment. In a circular geometry, they add actin, myosin, and fascin, allow them to bind, and then observe how the system evolves over time [2]. Fascin is a protein used for crosslinking and bundling actin filaments into long parallel structures, which is necessary for creating and stabilizing long, finger-like protrusions. The main variable they control is the quantity of actin and myosin added into the circular mold, which determines the density of the actomyosin gel. They then study how the gel changes over time. They found that the gel develops three distinct types of shapes depending on its thickness or density: dome-like, intermediate, and wrinkled/buckling, as the density decreases.
