Paper Summary: Self-assembled active actomyosin gels spontaneously curve and wrinkle similar to biological cells and tissues

Actin filaments are long chains that continuously assemble and disassemble through a dynamic process of polymerization and depolymerization. They are made up of spherical G‚Äëactin protein units, which polymerize using ATP (converted to ADP), a behavior well‚Äëestablished from in vivo experiments. These filaments interact with myosin proteins, which have two globular heads connected by twisted filamentous tails. Myosin motors ‚Äúwalk‚Äù along the actin filaments by cyclically attaching to and detaching from the G‚Äëactin subunits.

Together with intermediate filaments and microtubules, actin filaments form the cytoskeletal network of the cell, a fluid‚Äëlike yet structurally supportive system that maintains cell shape and holds the organelles in place (Watch Cytoskeleton;Sketchy MCAT and Cell Organelles: Drew Berry). Among these, microtubules are the largest filaments, actin filaments are the smallest, and the others of intermediate size are called intermediate filaments. When myosin motors move along actin filaments, they generate contractile forces, which drive cell migration, protrusion formation, cell division and membrane shaping. Because actomyosin contraction is one of the strongest mechanisms for converting chemical energy (ATP ‚Üí ADP) into mechanical motion, it forms the basis of cellular motility and remains a fascinating topic of study.

There have been many in vivo and in vitro studies that examine this aspect of actomyosin. In this paper, the authors study a simple system, a pure actomyosin gel, in an in vitro experiment. In a circular geometry, they add actin, myosin, and fascin, allow them to bind, and then observe how the system evolves over time [2]. Fascin is a protein used for crosslinking and bundling actin filaments into long parallel structures, which is necessary for creating and stabilizing long, finger-like protrusions. The main variable they control is the quantity of actin and myosin added into the circular mold, which determines the density of the actomyosin gel. They then study how the gel changes over time. They found that the gel develops three distinct types of shapes depending on its thickness or density: dome-like, intermediate, and wrinkled/buckling, as the density decreases. ¬†

If you‚Äôve ever seen a shrinking plastic sheet, it‚Äôs made of stretched polymers that were frozen in a straight position. When the sheet is heated, the polymer chains relax and become shorter, so the whole sheet shrinks (see Video 1). The heat gives energy that drives this process. The actomyosin disc works in a similar way but instead of heat, myosin motors provide the energy. These motors pull on actin filaments, causing the network to contract. Although the microscopic mechanism is different, the overall effect shrinking and deforming is quite similar to the shrinking plastic sheet.

In the disc, some areas contract more than others, especially because the top and bottom surfaces and the edges behave differently. This creates unequal stresses inside the disc, which lead to the bending or wrinkling shapes we see. Unlike the plastic sheet that becomes flat again after the heat is removed, the actomyosin disc stays in its domed or wrinkled shape because the myosin motors keep pulling as long as they have energy. The authors also showed that how thick or wide the disc is (its aspect ratio) decides which shape it takes.

Still, there are differences: in the actomyosin domes, the filaments line up differently near the edges than in the center, and the disc‚Äôs thickness also changes and these are details scientists are still studying.

Reflections

In ordinary sheets, we apply heat and they change their behavior. But in living cells, the same transformation happens from within . Their internal machinery, like the actomyosin network, can generate and control these forces by itself. Life is a beautiful and sophisticated form of self-organization, continuously at play on every scale.
