Paper Summary: Escape dynamics of confined undulating worms

October 10, 2025

California blackworm (*Lumbriculus variegatus*) movement ‚Äî source: YouTube

Escape is an interesting problem. Life exists in a complex environment, and over time it has found efficient ways to navigate through it. If we can understand those natural strategies, we can use them to build smarter materials and tools. Many of our designs, from planes to medical devices, are inspired by such living systems.

Peristaltic Crawling Adapted from Taylor & Francis Online.

The authors study one such system: Lumbriculus variegatus, also called the California blackworm, a common freshwater worm and a favorite food of fish (Figure 1). These worms show peristaltic motion when moving on hard surfaces (Figure 2) and undulatory motion when swimming in fluid (Figure 3). In their experiments, the worms often used a mix of both kinds of movement.

To explore how these worms escape confined spaces, the researchers built two circular chambers connected by a narrow channel made of acrylic. They observed how the worm‚Äôs head aligned with the boundaries, how its body followed, and what that revealed about its angular motion. They analyzed when the worm enters the channel, when it actually passes through, and what happens during that process. Their results showed that the body‚Äôs angular motion is correlated with its body angle but not with the head angle.

Undulatory motion in dogfish (source: SMU Propulsion)

They also used a simple active Brownian particle model to test whether random fluctuations alone could reproduce the escape dynamics. The model confirmed that sometimes, because of stochastic behavior, a particle can change its direction and show similar escape-time patterns as the worms.

Interestingly, even though the California blackworm has sensory nerves throughout its body, they were not strongly involved in the search for the opening. The search was mainly guided by the head.

Reflections

  • A full model is needed to capture the complete body dynamics of the worm. The head, being the decision-making part, can be modeled to move slower than the rest of the body. This slower motion allows it to sense, process, and choose direction, while the following segments respond faster.
