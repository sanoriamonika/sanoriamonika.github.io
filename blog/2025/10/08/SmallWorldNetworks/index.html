Imagine we are in old times, and you want to send a letter or a gift to someone you‚Äôve only heard of. How many people would it take to pass your message through to reach that person?

Stanley Milgram‚Äôs social experiments showed that, on average, you would need about six connections. This was the surprising result of one of Milgram‚Äôs most famous experiments, where he selected people from one part of the United States and asked them to send a letter to a stockbroker in Boston. The only condition was that they could forward the letter only to someone they personally knew, and that person would do the same. On average, it took about six people for the letter to reach the stockbroker in Boston. This was astonishing ‚Äî in a world with billions of people, we are only about six connections apart from each other. Milgram called this the ‚Äúsmall-world problem.‚Äù

From sociology to science: Watts and Strogatz brought this idea into the realm of physics and network theory. They noticed that earlier scientific models assumed systems were either completely random or perfectly ordered. They argued that in a completely random network, every node is close to many others, meaning the average distance between any two nodes is very small. In contrast, in a perfectly ordered network, every node is only connected locally ‚Äî to its nearest neighbors ‚Äî so distant nodes are far apart, leading to a very long average path length. However, they reasoned that real systems do not follow either extreme.

To test this, they created a circular lattice model where each node was connected to its nearby neighbors (about four on each side). Then, with a small probability p, they rewired some of these links ‚Äî cutting a few local connections and reconnecting them to distant nodes. They found that when p is small (around 0.1‚Äì0.2), the network still maintains a high clustering coefficient (strong local grouping), but the average path length drops sharply ‚Äî becoming as short as that of random networks. This was a surprising and important finding: a few long-range links are enough to make the whole network globally efficient while keeping local structure intact.

Watts and Strogatz verified this phenomenon in real-world networks ‚Äî the neural network of C. elegans, the collaboration network of Hollywood actors, and the Western U.S. power grid. All these networks showed the same property: a small-world structure ‚Äî where everything is locally connected but globally close. In essence, we live in a world which is huge, yet we are only a few steps apart from each other.
