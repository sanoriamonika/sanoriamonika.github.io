Cells that move on their own must first break their internal symmetry to decide which side will be the ‚Äúfront‚Äù and which will be the ‚Äúback.‚Äù Once this symmetry is broken, the cell can start moving in the chosen direction. But how does the cell make this decision? Scientists think there is a kind of ‚Äúsymmetry test‚Äù inside the cell ‚Äî a feedback between forming a protrusion (the leading edge) and establishing cell polarity (the internal front‚Äìback organization).

Inside the cell, many organelles work together to support this process. The Golgi apparatus produces and sends out proteins and vesicles that help the cell function and move. These vesicles often travel along an internal axis connecting the nucleus and the Golgi. So, researchers wondered:

  • Does the nucleus‚ÄìGolgi (NG) axis decide where the front of the cell will be,
  • or does the formation of a front (protrusion) determine how this axis aligns?

The role of the Golgi apparatus is like that of a post office. It receives proteins and lipids from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), modifies them, and sends them to where they are needed inside or outside the cell.

Key Players in the Feedback Mechanism

  • Protrusion dynamics: Protrusions are powered by the actin cytoskeleton. The small GTPase Cdc42 acts as a key regulator, promoting actin polymerization at the leading edge. Experimental evidence shows that artificially activating Cdc42 can direct the cell‚Äôs migration.__

  • Polarized trafficking: The Golgi apparatus, oriented toward the direction of migration, directs the flow of vesicles to the cell front.

  • Microtubules are essential for this process. When disrupted with the drug Nocodazole, cells could still form protrusions but lost their long-term directional persistence. The trafficking of Golgi-derived vesicles, marked by the protein Rab6, became random.

  • Integrin recycling: Integrins‚Äîproteins that form adhesions with the extracellular matrix‚Äîare critical cargo for polarized trafficking. They are recycled via endosomes and delivered back to the leading edge, providing the cell with traction to move forward. The dynamic regulation of integrin trafficking balances cell adhesion and migration.

The researchers developed a minimal computational model for persistent cell migration based on a positive feedback loop. It uses two coupled stochastic differential equations to describe the interaction between the cell‚Äôs internal polarity axis (NG axis) and its leading-edge protrusions. This model shows that the dynamic coupling between protrusive activity and polarized trafficking is sufficient to generate the persistent, directional migration observed in cells.

The NG axis does not predict the direction of movement but reorients once a direction is established. If the NG axis initially points elsewhere and a protrusion forms in a perpendicular direction, the model predicts that trafficking shifts first toward the new protrusion (after ~20 min), and then, over a longer timescale (1‚Äì3 hr), the NG axis rotates and aligns with it.
