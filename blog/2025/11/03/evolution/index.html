How does a cell survive? It takes energy, produces food for itself, and does everything necessary to stay alive. But why does it do it? I don‚Äôt know. How does it do it? A cell is like a continuously running, evolving machine and it never truly stops. Even when one cell is about to die, it divides to start another, often an improved copy that carries the accumulated experience and code of the previous cell through its DNA.

What keeps this machine running? What are the things this machine needs to keep running? All the chemical building blocks (like amino acids, lipids, nucleotides), energy sources to power reactions and movement, and a storage system for all instructions that tell it what to do and when to do it. Food provides both the raw materials and the energy (like glucose, fats, and proteins), while DNA acts as the cell‚Äôs instruction manual. Some components are not newly produced every time they are inherited directly from the parent cell, such as mitochondria and other organelles that help the new cell start functioning immediately. So, to function, a cell needs: (1) food, (2) a suitable environment with the right chemical conditions, and (3) a functioning genetic code with instructions and it‚Äôs ready to go.

How does it use these resources? Now, using these raw materials and chemicals in the exact right proportions and sequences must be a tedious task. So, our cells already have a plan, they ‚Äúknow‚Äù what kind of nutrients they will need, and their genetic code specifies how to convert these nutrients into useful forms. They do this through the processes of transcription (copying DNA instructions into RNA) and translation (converting RNA into proteins). Each protein produced performs a specific function from metabolism to repair ensuring the cell‚Äôs survival. DNA, therefore, contains the code for everything: for the processes that sustain life and, if misregulated, for those that can end it. It maintains the delicate balance of all parts that a living ‚Äúmachine‚Äù needs to survive.

The logic of survival and cooperation If a cell chose only its individual survival, cancer might seem like an ideal strategy, cell that keeps dividing and delays death. But this selfish behavior destroys the larger organism the ‚Äúhuman machine‚Äù and prevents genetic information from being passed on to offspring. So, it‚Äôs not an optimal strategy for the collective well-being of the multicellular organism.

From single cells to human complexity Humans are far more complex versions of the single-cell machine. We manage everything that began with simple prokaryotic cells. There are countless interactions and precise timings that must go right every fraction of a second for a living being to function. For you to move your foot or to smile even once, millions of molecular events have to occur in perfect order and yet they happen effortlessly. That‚Äôs how remarkable this system is.

Complexity, instructions, and growth

One of the arguments of this study is about how the length of the instructions (genes) relates to the complexity and growth of organisms. It‚Äôs easy to imagine that the more complex an organism, the more interactions it must coordinate ‚Äî and hence, the longer or richer its code must be. Our current understanding tells us that this code is written in the DNA (genome). (For more detail, see this blog: Understanding the Genome ) DNA can be broken into smaller functional units called genes, and each gene can produce, on average, about three proteins necessary for functioning. The authors argue that complexity increased over evolutionary time, and therefore, the average gene and protein length also increased with it. Indeed, they found that both gene and protein lengths grow with organismal complexity.

However, protein length stops increasing around the time when cells transitioned from prokaryotes to eukaryotes. This is because eukaryotic cells adopted a new ‚Äúalgorithmic rule‚Äù ‚Äî dividing the processes into two stages: transcription inside the nucleus and translation outside the nucleus. This separation dramatically increased the combinatorial possibilities of genetic regulation ‚Äî an $^nC_2$-type explosion of possible arrangements, far richer than the simple prokaryotic setup where everything happened in one space. Genes, meanwhile, evolved another strategy ‚Äî they began including non-coding regions, allowing for complex regulation without changing the core protein structure. The length of non-coding DNA keeps increasing with organismal complexity, reflecting this evolutionary shift toward information-based rather than size-based innovation.

Reflections

Life optimizes survival not by direct design, but by exploring diversity. Increasing gene and protein length enlarges the combinatorial possibilities, allowing new functional arrangements to emerge. Over billions of years, this process gives rise to complexity ‚Äî an evolutionary form of algorithmic optimization, where diversity itself becomes the strategy.
