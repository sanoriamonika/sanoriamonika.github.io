Biological Background

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL) is a cancer of the blood and bone marrow that specifically affects B lymphocytes. It is a slow-progressing disease in which B cells become abnormal (malignant), survive longer than normal, and accumulate in the bone marrow, lymph nodes, blood, and spleen.

Understanding how these malignant B cells sense their environment is essential for designing therapeutic strategies. Two major chemokine receptors‚ÄîCCR7 and CXCR4‚Äîplay a crucial role in guiding these cells through tissues. Therefore, researchers study how these receptors internalize, recycle, and signal in response to chemokine gradients such as CCL19/CCL21 (ligands for CCR7) and CXCL12 (ligand for CXCR4).

Central Question and Main Findings

Why do single CLL cells show chemorepulsion at high chemokine gradients, while clusters of CLL cells exhibit chemoattraction under the same conditions?**

This study combines experiments, coarse-grained mechanochemical modeling, and agent-based simulations to answer this question.

Key Findings

  1. Single-cell chemorepulsion occurs at high chemokine concentrations because:

    • Front‚Äìback asymmetry in CCR7 endocytosis is disrupted.

    • Nonlinear ERK signaling reduces receptor availability at the front.

    • The cell effectively loses its ability to sense the gradient at the leading edge.

  2. Clusters do not chemorepel at high gradients because:

    • Boundary cells undergo endocytosis, but core cells continuously replace them at the front.

    • This maintains fresh, receptor-rich cells at the leading edge.

    • Collective sensing averages out noise, producing robust chemoattraction.

  3. Cluster size is a critical parameter:

    Small clusters may still chemorepel, but large clusters always chemoattract because of this internal ‚Äúcell turnover‚Äù at the boundary.

Thus, size, endocytosis, and ERK signaling dynamics together determine whether malignant lymphocytes move toward or away from a chemokine source.

Mechanistic Insights and Broader Impact

Why This Matters

Chemorepulsion is a natural regulatory mechanism in immune cells. It prevents over-accumulation of cells in regions with extremely high chemokine concentrations and helps maintain tissue homeostasis.

However, in cancer:

  • Dysregulated endocytosis,

  • Abnormal ERK kinetics, and

  • Altered receptor recycling

can cause malignant cells to sense gradients incorrectly, contributing to improper migration and potentially aiding metastasis or lymph-node infiltration.

This work highlights a broader principle important in chemotaxis, pattern formation, and collective behavior:

The same signal can produce opposite outcomes depending on system size and internal dynamics.__

Limitations and Open Questions

  • The exact biochemical form of nonlinear ERK signaling is not measured; the model assumes a simplified nonlinear function.

  • Endocytosis and recycling timescales for CLL need more precise measurements.

  • More detailed single-cell signaling data (ERK dynamics, CCR7 turnover) would enable better quantitative matching.

  • Future work could extend the agent-based model to describe individual cell behavior more directly, not only clusters.

Techniques, Assays, and Analysis Pipeline

Mathematical / Computational Framework

  1. Coarse-Grained Single-Cell Model**

    • Describes receptor‚Äìligand dynamics with endocytosis, recycling, and a nonlinear ERK response.

    • Captures how high ligand concentrations disrupt front‚Äìback receptor asymmetry.

  2. Agent-Based Cluster Model**

    • Cells are arranged in a 2D circular cluster.

    • Only boundary cells sense the gradient and generate traction forces.

    • Traction is regulated by ERK activity.

    • Key parameters:

      • cluster size

      • chemokine gradient

      • ERK response

      • on/off rates for directional bias

      • boundary mechanical constraints

This model shows that core cell turnover maintains a fresh supply of receptor-rich cells at the front, allowing persistent chemoattraction at high gradients.

Experimental Techniques

  • Brightfield / wide-field microscopy to track migration of single cells and clusters.

  • Flow cytometry to quantify CCR7 surface levels before and after chemokine exposure.

  • Spinning-disk confocal microscopy for high-resolution receptor imaging and ligand uptake.

  • Fluorescent tagging of both CCR7 and CCL19 to visualize receptor‚Äìligand distributions.

  • Pharmacological inhibition (Dyn-2, Dynasore, clathrin siRNA) to block endocytosis:

    • Blocking endocytosis abolished chemorepulsion, confirming its role.

  • Luciferase siRNA served as a control.

Key Measurements

  • FMI plots (Forward Migration Index) showed:

    • Single-cell chemorepulsion at high gradients.

    • Cluster chemoattraction in the same conditions.

  • CCR7 internalization:

    • ~50% drop in surface CCR7 within 30 minutes.

    • This drop is prevented when endocytosis is inhibited.

  • Velocity:

    • Mean speed remains essentially constant; directionality changes, not speed.

Final Cleaned Summary (Short Version)

This paper explains why single CLL cells chemorepel at high chemokine concentrations while large clusters chemoattract. Nonlinear ERK-dependent endocytosis disrupts gradient sensing in single cells, leading to chemorepulsion. In clusters, continuous replacement of boundary cells by core cells restores receptor availability at the leading edge, enabling robust chemoattraction. Through a combination of experiments, coarse-grained modeling, and agent-based simulations, the study identifies endocytosis, ERK signaling, and cluster size as the key determinants of migratory fate.
