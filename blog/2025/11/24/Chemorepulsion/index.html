Paper Summary: Collective Cell Motility Promotes Chemotactic Prowess and Resistance to Chemorepulsion

November 24, 2025 • Updated November 24, 2025

Biological Background

CLL (Primary human malignant B lymphocytes) cells are malignant lymphocytes, which are a type of white blood cell and part of the immune system. These cells express many receptors, as shown in Figure 1, and respond to different external signals. The two most studied receptors are CCR7 and CXCR4 (the “R” stands for receptor, part before e.g., CC or CXC represents the families), which respond to the chemokines CCL19/21 and CXCL12, respectively. These chemokines act as homing signals for the lymph nodes and bone marrow. The presence of these receptors and the state they are in (for example, ligand-bound, unbound, or internalized), as well as the downstream signaling they initiate such as ERK activation and it is crucial for understanding and predicting CLL cell behavior in more detail.

Central Question and Main Findings

In this work, the authors wanted to understand the collective migration in CLL cancer cells by examining the dynamics of these receptors. The authors used primary CLL cells and focused on two chemokine receptors and their corresponding chemokines. They also used a theoretical model to verify the cluster state they observed experimentally. In their experimental system, they focused on:

  1. the migration of cells under a chemical gradient (CCL19),
  2. the expression of the tagged receptor CCR7 in the presence of tagged chemokines (CCL19/CCL21), and
  3. the receptor expression in leader cells to determine whether it differs from core cells.

The authors found that lymphocytes can chemotax as clusters and that their chemotactic efficiency is higher than that of single cells. In steep gradients, single cells show chemorepulsion, whereas follower cells within a cluster suppress CCL19-induced repulsion, move toward the center, and allow another cell to become the new leader. This mechanism keeps the cluster chemoattractive even when individual cells would show chemorepulsion to CCL19. They also found that clusters switch between different states random, rotational, and translational depending on cluster size and gradient strength.

Broader Impact

This result matters because it highlights a fundamental difference in how single cells and clusters interpret strong chemokine gradients. Single CLL cells undergo chemorepulsion at high gradient strength, which may represent a protective cue that tells them they are close to the lymph node and should stop moving. Large clusters, however, do not sense this “stop” signal and continue migrating, suggesting a possible reason why they are more invasive and able to occupy tissue niches aggressively. Understanding this distinction connects to broader ideas in chemotaxis, tissue invasion, and how collective behaviors can override single-cell regulatory mechanisms. At the same time, several open questions remain, including the need for continuous CCR7 surface measurements over the full one-hour period, clearer analysis of the timing of endocytosis versus recycling, and a better understanding of how receptor internalization in leader cells translates into specific ERK responses that regulate cluster decision-making.

Techniques, Assays, and Analysis Pipeline

They used a coarse-grained mechanochemical model of a cluster represented as a 2D circular disk, where only the boundary cells sense the chemokine gradient. The ERK signaling activity in these boundary cells generates traction forces that collectively drive cluster migration in response to a linear chemokine gradient. The key parameters of the model are: cluster size, chemokine concentration profile, boundary mechanical response, and the resulting traction forces. The model explains that within a cluster, noise is averaged out, making collective sensing more accurate. Because only boundary cells generate traction, their forces add up, producing a net force pointing toward higher chemokine concentration. Receptor internalization weakens the leader cell’s traction, causing it to eventually lose polarity and drop back into the cluster.

They studied CLL cancer cells in vitro under varying chemokine concentrations. Cell migration was observed in microfluidic devices using wide-field (brightfield) microscopy. To visualize receptor and ligand distributions under fluorescence, they used wide-field fluorescence microscopy, while spinning-disk confocal microscopy was used for high-resolution receptor imaging and 3D z-stack reconstruction. Flow cytometry was used to quantify CCR7 receptor levels on the cell surface.

Using brightfield/wide-field imaging, they found that at low chemokine gradients, clusters sense the gradient more effectively than single cells. At high gradients, single cells undergo chemorepulsion, whereas large clusters remain chemoattractive. Using the control Luc (luciferase siRNA) and inhibitors Dyn-2, Dynasore, and clathrin siRNA, they showed that CCR7 endocytosis is clathrin-mediated and dynamin-dependent, because chemorepulsion disappeared when either clathrin or dynamin function was inhibited. Flow cytometry showed that CCR7 surface levels drop to about 50% in 30 minutes under normal conditions, but this drop is blocked in inhibitor-treated cells.

Finally, velocity measurements revealed that average cell speed remains constant across conditions, although the Forward Migration Index (FMI) varies with gradient strength and cluster size.

Reflections

I think this result may help explain why large CLL clusters behave so invasively in tissues. Unlike single cells, clusters don’t seem to respond to very high chemokine concentrations by stopping or reversing, and this continued migration appears linked to how CCR7 internalization works at the front. The paper shows that CCR7 endocytosis is clathrin-mediated and dynamin-dependent, based on the effects of Dyn-2, dynasore, and clathrin siRNA. But some important measurements are missing, especially continuous CCR7 surface levels over the full hour and the timing of recycling back to the membrane. Understanding the complete endocytic cycle from internalization to recycling and its connection to ERK response and would give a much clearer mechanistic picture. How exactly they measured CCR7 at the 30-minute point and whether they performed ligand washout or any recycling analysis?
