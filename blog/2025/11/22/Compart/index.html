Paper Summary: Cellular compartmentalisation and receptor promiscuity as a strategy for accurate and robust inference of position during morphogenesis

November 22, 2025 ‚Ä¢ Updated November 22, 2025

Morphogenesis comes from the Greek morphƒì (‚Äúform‚Äù) and genesis (‚Äúcreation‚Äù), and refers to the physical, biochemical, and genetic processes that shape the structure and spatial organisation of living systems. To understand how form arises, we first need to know how cells interpret developmental cues, but these cues, morphogen signals, are noisy at every stage: during production, transport, receptor binding, and intracellular processing. Yet cells still manage to determine their exact position with remarkable accuracy. This paper investigates that central puzzle of morphogenesis: how does a cell reliably infer its position despite noisy and fluctuating morphogen signals?

In biological contexts, promiscuity refers to broad or non-selective interactions, and receptor promiscuity describes receptors that can bind multiple ligands or participate in overlapping pathways. Cellular compartmentalisation further refines how cells process signals by organising biochemical reactions into membrane-bound and non-membrane-bound compartments that keep processes efficient and separate. The authors combine analytical/information-theoretic modelling with computational optimisation to study how these mechanisms contribute to positional inference. Using experimental data from the Drosophila wing imaginal disc, they construct a Markov-state model of receptor binding, trafficking, and signalling, and then explore a large parameter space computationally to identify optimal and robust architectures. Their key result is that incorporating a second, non-signalling (‚Äúpromiscuous‚Äù) receptor branch along with multi-tier cellular trafficking pathways, dramatically improves both the accuracy and robustness of positional information decoding during morphogenesis.

Reflections

It amazes me how a very simple model can capture such a complex biological reality. Even though cells have thousands of receptors and an incredibly intricate internal system, a simplified model still gives intuitive insight into how they accurately sense their position despite all the noise.
