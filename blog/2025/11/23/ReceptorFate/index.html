Paper Summary: Opposite Fate of Endocytosed CCR7 and Its Ligands: Recycling versus Degradation

November 23, 2025 ‚Ä¢ Updated November 23, 2025

As I discussed in my old blog, endocytosis is a crucial process for the functioning of the cell. It acts as an important communication portal between the cell and its environment. Each cell carries receptor proteins on its surface, which function like locks in a communication system. These locks are opened by specific keys which are the ligands. Once a ligand binds to its receptor, different downstream mechanisms begin, and one of the major ones is endocytosis.

When a ligand binds to its receptor, it can trigger the formation of a membrane-bound vesicle known as an early endosome. This endosome then enters the intracellular trafficking system: some of its contents are delivered to lysosomes, where they are digested; some components may recycle back to the cell surface; and others are permanently degraded. Understanding these pathways gives us deeper clarity about how cells communicate, respond, and make decisions.

The receptor under study are CCR7 receptors and are found on many immune cells, and their ligands CCL19 and CCL21 are produced in lymph nodes to guide these cells. CCL21 creates long-range attraction, pulling cells from far away toward the lymph node, while CCL19 provides short-range, precise positioning once the cells arrive. Together, they coordinate the homing and localization of immune cells within the lymph node.

In this work, the authors asked a very specific question: What is the fate of the receptor and the ligand after endocytosis?Do they survive and return to the membrane, or are they degraded inside the cell?

More specifically, the authors set out to understand the fate of the CCR7 receptor and its ligands after endocytosis‚Äîspecifically, whether they survive and recycle or become degraded. To investigate this, they used an experimental setup where the receptor was tagged with a GFP molecule so that its location could be tracked throughout the entire endocytic cycle. For the ligand, they used an Fc-tag or a biotin tag, which allowed them to visualize the ligand using fluorescent antibodies or streptavidin-based detection. This dual-tagging strategy let them track both receptor and ligand independently and map out their complete intracellular journey. Using flow cytometry and imaging, they tracked receptor surface levels, ligand uptake, and the intracellular trafficking route. By analysing signal patterns from receptor-bound and ligand-bound tags, they were able to map the pathways involved and determine what happens at each stage of endocytosis.

Their experiments showed that CCR7 internalizes through the classical clathrin-mediated endocytic pathway. They performed three key tests: untreated cells, which migrated normally because CCR7 was fully available; cells exposed to high CCL19 for 30 minutes, where CCR7 was internalized and the cells completely lost migratory ability; and cells that were washed and allowed to recover for one hour, in which CCR7 reappeared on the surface and migration was restored. This demonstrated that the receptor is not degraded but instead recycles back to the membrane. In contrast, the ligands were tracked over time and found to be routed to lysosomes, where they were degraded rather than recycled.

Thus, the work reveals a clear dual fate: CCR7 receptors recycle, restoring migratory responsiveness, whereas CCL19/CCL21 ligands are degraded after internalization.

Reflections

I found it very interesting that CCL19 and CCL21 play such a central role in guiding cells, much like the homing effects seen in lymphocytes. In this paper, when cells were saturated with CCL19, they completely lost their migratory ability because all CCR7 receptors were internalized. What struck me was how long this process takes: after 30 minutes almost all receptors were gone from the surface, but after one hour most had recycled back.

In real systems, receptor binding, endocytosis, and recycling happen at the same time, creating a constant balance between how many receptors are being taken in and how many are returning to the membrane. This balance determines whether the cell can sense a gradient or not. It‚Äôs a dynamic and concentration-dependent process, and it raises interesting questions about how different cell types including the CLL cells I study, which show chemorepulsion and nterpret chemokine signals under varying conditions.
