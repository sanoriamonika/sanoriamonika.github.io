Paper Summary: Whole-body connectome of a segmented annelid larva

September 24, 2025

Neuroscience PaperSummary

How do we feel, react, and think? How does life work? We have been trying to answer these questions for centuries. Every time humanity learns a new mathematical trick or invents new tools, we gain something more that helps us answer in a little more depth. This paper is about another such milestone, which enhances our knowledge about the system, as we are now able to see all the neuron connections at once.

This paper presents huge data sets of the neuronal wiring map of a 3-day-old marine larva, called Platynereis dumerilii. Through large collaborations of many experts and advanced technologies, the authors were able to create a massive map of whole-body neuronal wiring using an electron microscope, referred to as a whole-body synaptic connectome. Here, synaptic refers to the junctions between nerve cells that transfer signals to each other, and connectome refers to the map of neural connections in the brain, detailing how neurons and brain regions are wired together. The massive map they created at this stage also classifies the cells present into 202 neuronal cell types and 92 non-neuronal cell types, and shows how they are connected to these neurons. To create this, they used 3-day-old Platynereis dumerilii larvae, around 130 µm in size. Why Platynereis? Because they are easy to maintain in the lab and have transparent bodies, which helps in visualizing their interior. Hence, scientists have been working with them for a long time to understand body functions and the neuronal system.

So, do I understand every conclusion of this project? Not yet. But I did enjoy seeing what we can explore today with collaboration and the technologies at hand. I also like that the authors have provided an interactive plugin, MoBIE (Resource 3), for their entire synaptic connectome, where you can see their actual data and even work with it.

Reflections

  • It also connects to the paper we discussed in yesterday’s post about river networks. This is a network of neurons but on a much larger scale; in reference to rivers, it is like the river network of the entire Earth, including not only fluvial systems but others as well.

  • It can also help us understand the connections and responses of neurons and specific cell types. We can shed light on the dependence of cell behaviour on long-range cell connections.
