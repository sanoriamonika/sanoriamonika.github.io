Dictyostelium discoideum, commonly known as a slime mold, is a fascinating organism.
Here‚Äôs how it differs from fungi (mushrooms):

  • Slime molds move (~1‚Äì2 cm/hr), while fungi do not.
  • Slime mold fruiting bodies are millimeters in size, while mushrooms are centimeters.
  • Slime molds are microorganisms that exist as single cells and as multicellular forms during their life cycle, usually in damp environments.

Food

Slime molds feed mostly on bacteria and decaying organic matter. They can surround and engulf particles in a process called phagocytosis, almost like a slow-motion hunt for food.

System of interest: Cellular Slime Mold

Cellular slime molds like Dictyostelium are special because they can live alone as single cells and then come together to act as a multicellular organism when food is scarce. This transition makes them an amazing system for studying how individual cells coordinate and communicate.

Why they are interesting to study

Slime molds are a perfect example of how simple rules at the level of individual cells can lead to complex, collective behavior. They move, sense gradients, and form organized structures, making them a natural model for understanding self-organization in living systems.

What interesting things are yet to understand and how it can relate to cancer cells

Scientists are still exploring how these cells communicate and make group decisions ‚Äî how they know when to move, stop, or form a structure. Similar questions appear in cancer biology, where individual cells also move collectively, sense chemical gradients, and change behavior in response to their surroundings.
